I came to PNW Climate Week curious how I might be able to put years of experience building software in service of the planet.

## Day 1

We crowdsourced what we noticed onto paper, and graphic recorder <a href="https://mollychidsey.com/" target="_blank">Molly Chidsey</a> drew our collective vision from those notes here:

I was happy to connect with Renee Rutz, who handles communications for the <a href="https://orclimatehub.org/" target="_blank">Oregon Climate Action Hub</a>, an amazing resource to help Oregonians find ways to get involved. I also shared lunch with some great folks including panelist Sherrie Villmark, Program Director at <a href="https://communityenergyproject.org/" target="_blank">Community Energy Project</a>, where they weatherize homes and bring community solar to low income households. I loved her response when I told her about my work building software for foster families: "the best way to help children is by helping the planet!"

Later that evening I went over to Sunnyside Community Center and experienced conservation at the Minam River Wildlife Area through a VR headset before viewing <a href="https://othersideofthehillmovie.com/" target="_blank">Other Side of the Hill</a>, thanks to another rad local org, <a href="https://www.nwdoc.org" target="_blank">NW Documentary</a>. The film amplifies rural community leaders in Eastern Oregon finding hope in a changing landscape, with a filmmaker Q&A after. Data centers came up here and again on the farm tour the next day, as a potential buyer for farmers who have trouble selling power back to the utilities.

## Day 2

Sun, Soil & Sovereignty was an agrivoltaics tour with SHEBA Farm at Oregon State's North Willamette Research and Extension Center in Aurora, where the same acre grows food and generates power. Under the tent, <a href="https://farmsolarfit.org" target="_blank">Farm Solar Fit</a> walked through the system they are building for Oregon and Washington farmers. It weighs three options over a 20 year horizon: keep farming as is, lease the land for solar, or go agrivoltaic. The math runs on the actual soil, sun, and water numbers for a parcel instead of generic estimates.

OSU professor <a href="https://agsci.oregonstate.edu/users/chad-higgins" target="_blank">Chad Higgins</a> reframed sunlight as something fungible because panels enable farmers to create surplus power. C3 and C4 plants differ in how they convert photons, so every crop has its own saturation point. Shade from the panels cuts water demand and heat stress, and because plants grow in degree days, you can even manage the bloom calendar. Oregon happens to sit at the perfect latitude where this practice helps both the plants and the economics. The golden rule is don't block the row, and stringing cables across the front is one of the easiest ways to break it!

Itanna and Dr. Ardelia Clarke could not have been more welcoming. Itanna leads <a href="https://shebafarm.com" target="_blank">SHEBA Farm</a>, activating 10 acres of underutilized Willamette Valley farmland for beginning and underrepresented farmers. Dr. Clarke, also from SHEBA, runs <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/nerd-n-out/" target="_blank">Nerd'N Out</a>, which brings PhD level rigor to frontline communities and tribal nations who rarely get access to it. Jana Gastellum came out for the tour her org sponsored, as Executive Director of the <a href="https://oeconline.org/" target="_blank">Oregon Environmental Council</a>.

<a href="https://www.womenincleantechsustainability.org/" target="_blank">Women in Cleantech and Sustainability</a> and <a href="https://futureclimatecollective.org/" target="_blank">Future Climate Collective</a> put on a screening of Future Council, a doc about kids taking on corporate and political leaders over climate accountability. Afterward, Portland's Chief Sustainability Officer Vivian Satterfield moderated a youth panel with Sage Dalke from the Collective, Shubh Narayan from the city's Sustainability and Climate Commission, and Twyla Metcalfe, an environmental studies major at Lewis & Clark.

Erin Kester, who was volunteering that night, was so friendly and went out of her way to make me feel at home. It turns out she worked on the big wind farm offshore of Humboldt, where I live now, and we had been neighbors of a sort long before we ever met. Erin leads external affairs at <a href="https://www.esvolta.com" target="_blank">esVolta</a>, building utility scale battery storage, after 15 years shaping energy policy across the West.

## Day 3

<a href="https://www.rebuildingcenter.org" target="_blank">ReBuilding Center</a> hosted Reimagining & Repurposing, where a longtime Portland craftsman walked us through the cleaning, repair, and restoration that makes reuse possible. I love picking scrapyards and finding deals at thrift shops, keeping what we already have out of the landfill a while longer. It might be the least glamorous corner of the circular economy and the most useful one.

I also attended a fireside chat with <a href="https://www.depave.org" target="_blank">Depave</a> at the Glass Lab, hosted by <a href="https://www.knotstudio.com/" target="_blank">Knot Studio</a>. Since 2008 Depave has been tearing out pavement nobody needed and putting living, permeable ground back in its place. They walked us through GIS analysis of tree cover that surfaced the hottest parts of the city, which is how they find the neighborhoods that need shade most. It is <a href="/food-forests-and-natural-building/">permaculture</a> at city scale.

Depave mentioned a possible collaboration with the <a href="https://www.pdx.edu/indigenous-nations-studies/psu-oak-savanna" target="_blank">PSU Oak Savanna</a>, an urban restoration site where students have been planting white oak, camas, and serviceberry to bring back native plants and traditional Indigenous practices. I met Brittany Gadbury from Portland Parks & Rec's <a href="https://www.portland.gov/trees" target="_blank">Urban Forestry</a> team, who pointed me toward the <a href="https://www.portlandplacemaking.org/" target="_blank">Portland Placemaking Coalition</a> and <a href="https://www.treesforlifeoregon.org" target="_blank">Trees for Life Oregon</a>, both of which I am excited to learn more about.

This is just a tiny fraction of events throughout the entire week across seven cities, from Vancouver, BC to Bend. Even that small sample blew me away with the scale and diversity of the community. I met a wide variety of participants, from professors and craftsmen to executive directors and urban foresters. I came in wondering where my experience might serve, and the week kept answering: solar arrays, battery storage, a VR headset, data centers, GIS maps finding the blocks that need shade. Software is vital for the health of our planet, and I would love to see more engineers out there. Let's co-create a deeper connection between tech and climate action in 2027!
