## Day 1

PNW Climate Week kicked off with a full day of panels and workshops around "Empowering Local Climate Action: Education, Policy and Practice". It opened with a guided visualization to imagine 2035 through each of our senses. We crowdsourced what we noticed onto paper, and graphic recorder <a href="https://mollychidsey.com/" target="_blank">Molly Chidsey</a> drew our collective vision from those notes, live.

I was happy to connect with Renee Rutz, who handles communications for the <a href="https://orclimatehub.org/" target="_blank">Oregon Climate Action Hub</a>, an amazing resource to help Oregonians find climate work. I also shared lunch with some great folks including panelist Sherrie Villmark, Program Director at <a href="https://communityenergyproject.org/" target="_blank">Community Energy Project</a>, where they weatherize homes and bring community solar to low income households. ## Day 2

Sun, Soil & Sovereignty was an agrivoltaics tour with SHEBA Farm at Oregon State's North Willamette Research and Extension Center in Aurora, where the same acre grows food and generates power. Under the tent, <a href="https://farmsolarfit.org" target="_blank">Farm Solar Fit</a> walked through the tool they are building for Oregon and Washington farmers. It weighs three options over a 20 year horizon: keep farming as is, lease the land for solar, or go agrivoltaic. The math runs on the actual soil, sun, and water numbers for a parcel instead of generic estimates.

OSU professor <a href="https://agsci.oregonstate.edu/users/chad-higgins" target="_blank">Chad Higgins</a> reframed sunlight as something you meter out because it is fungible. C3 and C4 plants differ in how they convert photons, so every crop has its own saturation point. The panels enable farmers to sell the surplus as power while the shade cuts water demand and heat stress. Since plants grow in degree days, you can even manage the bloom calendar. Oregon happens to sit at the perfect latitude where this practice helps both the plants and the economics. The golden rule is don't block the row, and stringing cables across the front is one of the easiest ways to break it!

Itanna and Dr. Ardelia Clarke could not have been more welcoming. Itanna leads <a href="https://shebafarm.com" target="_blank">SHEBA Farm</a>, activating 10 acres of underutilized Willamette Valley farmland for beginning and underrepresented farmers. Dr. Clarke, also from SHEBA, runs <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/nerd-n-out/" target="_blank">Nerd'N Out</a>, which brings PhD level rigor to frontline communities and tribal nations who rarely get access to it. Jana Gastellum came out for the tour her org sponsored, as Executive Director of the <a href="https://oeconline.org/" target="_blank">Oregon Environmental Council</a>. I'm looking forward to learning more about all of it!

<a href="https://www.womenincleantechsustainability.org/" target="_blank">Women in Cleantech and Sustainability</a> and <a href="https://futureclimatecollective.org/" target="_blank">Future Climate Collective</a> put on a screening of Future Council, a doc about kids taking on corporate and political leaders over climate accountability. Afterward, Portland's Chief Sustainability Officer Vivian Satterfield moderated a youth panel with Sage Dalke from the Collective, Shubh Narayan from the city's Sustainability and Climate Commission, and Twyla Metcalfe, an environmental studies major at Lewis & Clark.

## Day 3

<a href="https://www.rebuildingcenter.org" target="_blank">ReBuilding Center</a> hosted Reimagining & Repurposing, where a longtime Portland picker and craftsman walked us through the cleaning, repair, and restoration that makes reuse possible. It might be the least glamorous corner of the circular economy and the most useful one, keeping what we already have out of the landfill a while longer.

I also attended a fireside chat with <a href="https://www.depave.org" target="_blank">Depave</a> at the Glass Lab, hosted by <a href="https://www.knotstudio.com/" target="_blank">Knot Studio</a>. Since 2008 Depave has been tearing out pavement nobody needed and putting living, permeable ground back in its place, starting with the neighborhoods holding the least greenspace. It is <a href="/food-forests-and-natural-building/">permaculture</a> at city scale.

One of the speakers mentioned a possible collaboration with the <a href="https://www.pdx.edu/indigenous-nations-studies/psu-oak-savanna" target="_blank">PSU Oak Savanna</a>, an urban restoration site where students have been planting white oak, camas, and serviceberry to bring back native plants and traditional Indigenous practices.
